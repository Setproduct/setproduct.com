## 2. Target audience (Audience B)

Profile:
→ Mid-level designers, 4-8 years of experience
→ Already working in a corporate UX/Product role (not necessarily FAANG)
→ Some success, but feeling a ceiling or discontent
→ Considering alternative paths, but not trying to "quit tomorrow"
→ Have financial obligations, not 20-year-olds with a zero-risk profile
→ Skeptical of "quit your job and freelance" hype
→ Want a nuanced, mature conversation

This audience WANTS:
→ Honest tone, no hype
→ Acknowledgment that the corporate path is valid
→ Practical steps that are doable WITHOUT quitting
→ Examples that compound over years
→ Respect for their current position

This audience REJECTS:
→ "Just quit and follow your passion"
→ Influencer-style success theater
→ Framing corporate work as inherently bad

## 4. Six commenter summaries

### 4.1. Wintermute424, Principal Designer, big tech, $300k+

Started in UX, became the "go-to person" for "what's going on" information across the org. Promoted to lead UX naturally. Got into special projects, expanding the role over 10 years. Core advice: learn your domain, product, organization, and people deeply. Become trusted to handle things end-to-end, the kind of person who gets put in the "this will be handled well" mental box. Focus on outcomes rather than the letter of your job role. Step out of your lane to do work owned by others, but do it as a friend, not territorially. Make allies, not enemies. People should make good noises about you when you are not in the room.

### 4.2. raindownthunda, clearly senior, role unstated

Lists relationships as #1. Flexibility and willingness to deviate from dogmatic process matters. Compromise where needed but inject expertise. Kill ego but no self-deprecation. Volunteer first for big opportunities, say no to 90 percent of the rest. Always follow through. Do less so you can do better. Be opinionated, bring energy. Admit when wrong. Help your boss and their peers succeed. Set up 1:1s with more senior people across the org. Share work more broadly than feels comfortable. Mentor juniors. Camera on during remote calls.

### 4.3. justadadgame, veteran, role unstated

Do not stay in your lane. Do not wait for permission. Understand the bigger picture: business goals, operations, engineering and people costs. Make calls yourself. Tell people what needs to get done. Can reach principal through deep design alone, but the path is harder. Focus on what moves users and on optimizing the way you get there. Soft skills are the multiplier.

### 4.4. Illah, non-traditional path

Started at a $12 per hour internship at a 25-person startup, went through IDEO, ended up in big tech leadership. Frames the non-traditional path as a competitive advantage. Adaptability and comfort with ambiguity matter more than corporate fit. Stayed creative outside work: art, lectures, publications. The "windy road" builds wisdom that a straight corporate path cannot. Adds the honest caveat "sample size of one but..."

### 4.5. krullulon, VP big tech, 17 years junior to VP

The most structured breakdown.

→ Early career: identified personal strengths (intuitive usability heuristics) and built a brand around them.
→ Mid career: understood that design's role is to build business value just as much as PM's role is to build business value. Most design peers felt unheard about UX. This focus on business value was the key differentiator.
→ Transition to Director: work shifts from delivering excellent UX to solving organizational problems. UX practitioners are often better at process rigor than PM or engineering. People leadership matters: succession planning, talent, performance management, motivating both good performers and 10x performers. Manage poor performance aggressively and early.
→ Transition to VP: a secondary spotlight skill outside design (his was Marketing). Becomes a Swiss Army knife bridging gaps no one else covers. Cross-functional leadership relationships compound.

### 4.6. Stressisnotgood, Principal Designer, F500, $700k TC

Bullet list:
→ Learn how to present (clarity, simplicity above all)
→ Learn the politics game
→ Build alliances, connect with other teams
→ Do not fear being wrong
→ Drive the work yourself
→ Self-initiate projects that become the roadmap
→ Balance work and life, say no to things
→ Be good at what you do (last item, with a winking emoji)

## 6. Five extracted skills (synthesized)

### ❶ Operating without a job description
→ Source: Wintermute424, raindownthunda, justadadgame, Stressisnotgood (4/6)
→ Reframe in post: ownership of outcomes beyond formal scope.

### ❷ Relationships as multipliers, not networking
→ Source: Wintermute424, raindownthunda, krullulon, Stressisnotgood (4/6)
→ Reframe: alliances built through helping others succeed, not transactional connection-making.

### ❸ Storytelling as career infrastructure
→ Source: Wintermute424 (echoing reputation), raindownthunda (share broadly), Illah (lectures/publications), Stressisnotgood (presentation) (4/6)
→ Reframe: communication that compounds beyond a single meeting.

### ❹ Translating craft into business
→ Source: Wintermute424 (outcomes), justadadgame (business costs), krullulon (business value as central insight) (3/6)
→ Reframe: connecting design work to financial impact in language non-designers respect.

### ❺ Selective ambition as a filter
→ Source: raindownthunda (90% no), Stressisnotgood (say no) (2/6 only, but qualitatively strong)
→ Reframe: depth through subtraction, not coverage through volume.

## 7. Gaps in the data (Roman's value-add space)

These themes are NOT mentioned by any of the 6 commenters, even though they apply to everyone:

→ Personal brand outside the company
→ Productizing expertise (UI kits, courses, templates)
→ Public writing as a career multiplier
→ Diversified income streams
→ Building an audience instead of a network
→ Compounding equity in your own work, not company stock
